Indication

Stroke Prevention in Non-Valvular Atrial Fibrillation

Thromboguard is indicated to reduce the risk of stroke and systemic embolism in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation (NVAF). It represents a new class of anticoagulant that selectively targets factor XIa.

By preserving the extrinsic coagulation pathway, Thromboguard delivers effective thrombosis prevention while fundamentally reducing the risk of bleeding complications that limit existing anticoagulants.

Mechanism of Action

Selective Factor XIa Inhibition

Thromboguard targets factor XIa in the intrinsic coagulation pathway with high selectivity, preserving hemostatic function by leaving the extrinsic pathway intact. This results in effective thrombosis prevention with minimal bleeding enhancement.

Intrinsic Pathway Target

Selectively inhibits activated factor XI (FXIa), a key amplifier of thrombin generation in the intrinsic coagulation cascade, reducing pathological clot formation at its source.

Preserved Hemostasis

Leaves the extrinsic (tissue factor) pathway fully intact, maintaining the body's natural hemostatic response to injury while preventing pathological thrombosis.

Lower Bleeding Risk

The unique mechanism delivers a fundamentally safer anticoagulant profile, with 47% less major bleeding and 75% fewer intracranial hemorrhages compared to warfarin.

Dosing in Special Populations

Moderate Renal Impairment (CrCl 30-50)

No dose adjustment required. Standard 40 mg BID maintained with periodic renal function monitoring.

Severe Renal Impairment (CrCl 15-30)

Reduce to 20 mg BID. Monitor renal function closely. Avoid if CrCl <15 mL/min or on dialysis.

Hepatic Impairment

No adjustment for mild-moderate impairment (Child-Pugh A/B). Avoid in moderate-severe with coagulopathy (Child-Pugh B/C).

Elderly Patients (≥75 years)

No specific dose adjustment. Age alone does not require reduced dosing, though bleeding risk increases with age.

Important Safety Information

CONTRAINDICATIONS: Thromboguard is contraindicated in patients with active pathological bleeding; in patients with severe hypersensitivity to rivaclixin maleate or any component of the product; in patients with CrCl <15 mL/min or on dialysis; in patients with prosthetic heart valves or hemodynamically significant valvular disease; in patients with moderate to severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh B or C) associated with coagulopathy.

WARNINGS AND PRECAUTIONS:

  • Risk of Bleeding: Thromboguard increases the risk of bleeding and can cause serious and potentially fatal bleeding. Promptly evaluate signs or symptoms of blood loss. Discontinue in patients with active pathological hemorrhage. Major bleeding occurred in 1.4% of patients (vs. 2.7% warfarin).
  • Spinal/Epidural Anesthesia or Puncture: Epidural or spinal hematomas may occur in patients treated with Thromboguard who are receiving neuraxial anesthesia or undergoing spinal puncture. These hematomas may result in long-term or permanent paralysis.
  • Premature Discontinuation: Premature discontinuation of any oral anticoagulant, including Thromboguard, in the absence of adequate alternative anticoagulation increases the risk of thrombotic events. If Thromboguard is discontinued for a reason other than pathological bleeding, consider coverage with another anticoagulant.
  • Renal Impairment: Dose reduction to 20 mg BID recommended for CrCl 15-30 mL/min. Avoid use in CrCl <15 mL/min. Periodically assess renal function and adjust dose accordingly.

ADVERSE REACTIONS: The most common adverse reaction was bleeding. Major bleeding: 1.4% vs. 2.7% (warfarin). Intracranial hemorrhage: 0.12% vs. 0.48% (warfarin). GI bleeding: 1.1% vs. 1.8% (warfarin). No routine coagulation monitoring required.

DRUG INTERACTIONS: Combined P-gp and strong CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers may increase/decrease rivaclixin exposure. Avoid concomitant use with other anticoagulants except during transition periods. Use caution with antiplatelet agents and NSAIDs due to additive bleeding risk.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Thromboguard differ from other oral anticoagulants?

Thromboguard selectively inhibits factor XIa in the intrinsic coagulation pathway, unlike warfarin (multiple factors) or direct oral anticoagulants like DOACs (factor Xa or thrombin). By preserving the extrinsic pathway, it maintains hemostatic function, resulting in 47% less major bleeding compared to warfarin.

Is INR monitoring required with Thromboguard?

No. Thromboguard does not require routine INR monitoring or any regular coagulation testing. Its pharmacokinetic profile provides predictable, consistent anticoagulation with fixed dosing. Periodic renal function assessment is recommended to ensure appropriate dosing.

How should patients be transitioned from warfarin to Thromboguard?

Discontinue warfarin and start Thromboguard when the INR falls below 2.0. No bridging with parenteral anticoagulation (e.g., heparin) is required during the transition. The GUARDIAN-SWITCH study confirmed this approach is safe and effective.
