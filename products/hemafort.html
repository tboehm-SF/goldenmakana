Indication

AML in Elderly/Unfit Patients

Hemafort is indicated for the treatment of newly diagnosed acute myeloid leukemia (AML) in adults aged 75 years or older, or those who have comorbidities that preclude the use of intensive induction chemotherapy.

In combination with azacitidine, Hemafort provides a chemotherapy-free option that targets the BCL-2 survival pathway, offering meaningful remission rates and improved overall survival in a patient population with historically poor outcomes.

Mechanism of Action

Targeted Apoptosis Restoration

Hemafort is a selective BCL-2 inhibitor that restores the intrinsic apoptotic pathway in leukemic cells. By displacing pro-apoptotic proteins from BCL-2, it triggers mitochondrial-mediated cell death selectively in BCL-2-dependent AML blasts.

BCL-2 Selective Binding

Binds directly to the BH3 domain of BCL-2 protein, displacing pro-apoptotic proteins BIM and BAX, restoring the intrinsic apoptotic pathway in leukemic blasts.

Mitochondrial Pathway Activation

Releases sequestered pro-apoptotic proteins, triggering mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP), cytochrome c release, and caspase cascade activation.

Synergy with Azacitidine

Hypomethylating agent azacitidine primes leukemic cells by upregulating BH3-only proteins and sensitizing them to BCL-2 inhibition, creating synthetic lethality.

How Hemafort Works in AML

1

BCL-2 Inhibition

Blocks survival signals keeping leukemic cells alive by occupying the BH3-binding groove of BCL-2

2

Pro-Apoptotic Release

BIM/BAX freed from BCL-2, activating BAK/BAX pore formation on the outer mitochondrial membrane

3

Mitochondrial Permeabilization

Cytochrome c released from mitochondria, caspase cascade initiated leading to irreversible cell death

4

Leukemic Cell Death

Selective apoptosis of BCL-2-dependent AML blasts, sparing normal hematopoietic cells

Clinical Evidence

VIALE-A Phase 3 Trial

A pivotal randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 430 adults with newly diagnosed AML demonstrated Hemafort plus azacitidine significantly improved remission rates and overall survival compared to azacitidine alone.

Warnings & Precautions

Tumor Lysis Syndrome

TLS including fatal events has been reported. Mandatory ramp-up dosing schedule with hydration and prophylaxis required. Monitor labs at each dose increase.

Neutropenia

Grade 3-4 neutropenia in 38% of patients. Monitor CBC before each cycle. Dose interrupt or reduce as needed to manage cytopenias.

Infections

Serious infections in 44% of patients. Monitor for signs of infection. Consider antimicrobial prophylaxis. Withhold for Grade 3-4 infections.

Special Populations & Considerations

TLS Prophylaxis

Mandatory hydration (1.5-2L daily) and allopurinol starting 2-3 days prior to first dose. Monitor chemistry at 6-8 and 24 hours at each ramp-up dose.

CYP3A4 Interactions

Reduce dose by at least 50% with moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors. Concomitant use with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors is contraindicated during ramp-up.

Hepatic Adjustment

Reduce dose by 50% in patients with moderate hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh B). Avoid use in severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh C).

Dose Modifications

Interrupt for Grade 4 hematologic toxicity or Grade 3-4 non-hematologic toxicity. Resume at same or reduced dose after recovery to Grade 1 or baseline.

Important Safety Information

CONTRAINDICATIONS: Hemafort is contraindicated in patients with hypersensitivity to venetoclax-mkna or any component of the formulation. Concomitant use with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors at initiation and during the ramp-up phase is contraindicated.

WARNINGS AND PRECAUTIONS:

  • Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS): TLS, including fatal events, has occurred in patients treated with Hemafort. Assess TLS risk based on tumor burden. Initiate prophylaxis with hydration and anti-hyperuricemics prior to first dose. Use dose ramp-up schedule. Monitor blood chemistry at 6-8 hours and 24 hours at each ramp-up dose increase.
  • Neutropenia: Grade 3-4 neutropenia occurred in 38% of Hemafort-treated patients. Monitor complete blood counts prior to each cycle and as clinically indicated. Dose interruptions or reductions may be required.
  • Infections: Serious infections, including fatal sepsis and pneumonia, occurred in 44% of patients. Monitor for signs and symptoms of infection. Consider antimicrobial prophylaxis. Withhold Hemafort for Grade 3-4 infections until resolution.
  • Drug Interactions: Venetoclax-mkna is a CYP3A4 substrate. Avoid strong CYP3A4 inhibitors during ramp-up. Reduce dose with moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors. Avoid strong CYP3A4 inducers. Avoid concomitant P-gp substrates with narrow therapeutic indices.

ADVERSE REACTIONS: The most common adverse reactions (≥20%) were nausea (42%), neutropenia (38%), diarrhea (35%), and thrombocytopenia (30%).

MONITORING: Blood chemistry for TLS at ramp-up. CBC before each cycle. Liver function tests periodically. Monitor for signs of infection throughout treatment.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How is tumor lysis syndrome prevented with Hemafort?

TLS prevention requires a multi-faceted approach: a mandatory 5-day dose ramp-up schedule (100mg, 200mg, 400mg), adequate hydration (1.5-2L daily beginning 2-3 days prior to first dose), and anti-hyperuricemic prophylaxis with allopurinol. Blood chemistry including potassium, uric acid, phosphorus, calcium, and creatinine should be monitored at 6-8 hours and 24 hours at each dose escalation step. Patients should be hospitalized for the first dose if they are high-risk for TLS based on tumor burden.

Does Hemafort need to be taken with food?

Yes, Hemafort tablets should be taken with a meal and water. Taking venetoclax-mkna with food increases oral bioavailability to approximately 50%. Without food, absorption is significantly reduced, which may lead to subtherapeutic drug levels. Patients should take their dose at approximately the same time each day with a standard meal, ideally with their largest meal of the day.

How long does treatment with Hemafort continue?

Treatment with Hemafort in combination with azacitidine should continue until disease progression, loss of response, or unacceptable toxicity. In the VIALE-A trial, the median duration of treatment was approximately 7.6 months. Some patients achieving deep remission have continued therapy for over 2 years. The decision to continue, modify, or discontinue therapy should be individualized based on response assessment, tolerability, and the patient's overall clinical status.
